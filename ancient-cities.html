For over fifteen centuries, the northern plains and central highlands of Sri Lanka were the center of a brilliant Buddhist civilization. The sacred cities of Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, and Kandy served as seats of power for successive dynasties.

From the world's tallest brick structures to the Temple of the Sacred Tooth Relic, these kingdoms represent the zenith of ancient South Asian culture, engineering, and spirituality.

Anuradhapura

Kingdom Founded c.380 BC — Capital for 1,400 Years

The First Sacred Capital

Anuradhapura was the first capital of ancient Sri Lanka and remains one of the world's major Buddhist pilgrimage sites. Founded in the 4th century BC, it grew into a sprawling metropolis centered around massive stupas and monastic complexes.

The city's layout was a masterpiece of urban planning, integrating spiritual monuments with an advanced hydraulic system of man-made reservoirs (wewas) and canals. These waters sustained a population that reached into the hundreds of thousands at its peak.

Today, the ruins offer a window into a time when Sri Lanka was a beacon of Buddhist learning, attracting scholars and pilgrims from across the Silk Road.

Ruwanwelisaya Stupa

📍 8.3490°N 80.3965°E · Anuradhapura

A hemispherical structure and a marvel of ancient engineering, standing as one of the world's tallest monuments of antiquity.

Sri Maha Bodhi

📍 8.3453°N 80.3961°E · Anuradhapura

The oldest documented tree in the world, grown from a sapling of the original Bodhi tree under which Buddha attained enlightenment.

Jetavanaramaya

📍 8.3511°N 80.4003°E · Anuradhapura

Once the third tallest structure in the ancient world, after the Great Pyramids, this massive brick stupa remains a breathtaking sight.

Thuparamaya

📍 8.3480°N 80.3950°E · Anuradhapura

The first Buddhist stupa built in Sri Lanka after the introduction of Buddhism, known for its elegant concentric stone pillars.

Twin Ponds (Kuttam Pokuna)

📍 8.3522°N 80.3978°E · Anuradhapura

Considered one of the best specimen of bathing tanks in ancient Sri Lanka, showcasing sophisticated filtration and drainage.

Polonnaruwa

Medieval Capital c.993–1310 AD

Gal Vihara

📍 7.9420°N 81.0020°E · Polonnaruwa

A rock temple of the Buddha where four colossal statues were carved out of a single granite rock face.

Parakrama Samudra

📍 7.9385°N 80.9893°E · Polonnaruwa

A massive man-made lake so large it was named the "Sea of Parakrama," a testament to ancient irrigation mastery.

Lankatilaka

📍 7.9318°N 80.9931°E · Polonnaruwa

An enormous brick image house with walls soaring 17 meters high, housing a headless but still imposing Buddha statue.

Vatadage

📍 7.9397°N 80.9998°E · Polonnaruwa

A circular relic house featuring intricate stone carvings and guard stones, considered one of the most beautiful ruins in the country.

Royal Palace of Parakramabahu

📍 7.9371°N 80.9971°E · Polonnaruwa

A multi-story structure that once boasted 1,000 rooms, now standing as impressive brick ruins of a royal past.

The Medieval Stronghold

After the fall of Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa became the second capital of Sri Lanka. It reached its zenith under King Parakramabahu I, who transformed the city into a magnificent garden city and irrigation hub.

Unlike the sprawling nature of Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa is more compact, with its major monuments clustered together, showcasing a blend of Buddhist and Hindu architectural influences.

The city's preservation is remarkable, allowing visitors to walk through the royal council chambers, library, and sacred quadrangles almost as they appeared eight centuries ago.

Kandy

City of the Sacred Tooth · Last Royal Capital 1592–1815

"Whoever holds the relic of the Tooth of the Buddha, holds sovereignty over the whole island of Lanka."

— Mahavamsa, Ancient Sri Lankan Chronicle
The Last Stronghold

A Kingdom in the Mountains

Kandy, known to locals as Maha Nuwara (The Great City), was the last royal capital of Sri Lanka. Nestled among the emerald hills of the central highlands, it remained an independent kingdom long after the coastal regions fell to European colonial powers.

The city's natural fortification by mountains and the Mahaweli River allowed the Kandyan kings to preserve their unique culture, arts, and political autonomy for over two centuries against the Portuguese and Dutch.

Even today, Kandy remains the spiritual heart of the nation — a place where the traditions of the ancient past continue to breathe in the modern world.

Its layout, centered around the picturesque Kandy Lake and the sacred temple complex, reflects a harmonious blend of nature and royal urban planning.

Chronicles of the Hill Capital

History Timeline

4th Century AD

Tooth Relic Arrives in Sri Lanka

Brought from Kalinga, India, hidden in the hair of Princess Hemamala, the relic became a symbol of divine right to rule.

1592

Kandy Becomes Capital

King Vimaladharmasuriya I established Kandy as the royal seat, bringing the Tooth Relic to the city for protection.

1600s–1700s

Resistance Against Colonial Powers

The kingdom successfully repelled numerous invasions by the Portuguese and Dutch, utilizing the rugged terrain.

1765

Current Temple Complex Built

King Kirti Sri Rajasinha oversaw the construction of much of the temple complex seen today, showcasing classic Kandyan architecture.

1815

The Kandyan Convention

The kingdom was finally ceded to the British Empire through a treaty, ending over 2,300 years of monarchical rule in Sri Lanka.

1988

UNESCO World Heritage Designation

The sacred city of Kandy was recognized for its immense cultural value and historical significance to the Buddhist world.

Temple of the Sacred Tooth Relic

The Sacred Relic 📍 7.2936°N 80.6413°E · Kandy

Enshrined in seven golden caskets, the tooth of the Buddha is the ultimate object of veneration for millions.

Architecture

The "Dalada Maligawa" features iconic Kandyan elements like the octagonal tower (Pattirippuwa) and moonstones.

The Museum Complex

Housing ancient royal artifacts and a record of the global significance of the Tooth Relic throughout history.

Daily Puja Ceremonies

Ancient rituals performed thrice daily by monks, accompanied by traditional drumming and horns.

Political & Spiritual Power

Historically the palladium of the state; today it remains central to Sri Lankan national identity.

Living Heritage

The Grandest Pageants

Kandy is famous for its vibrant festivals, the most spectacular being the Esala Perahera, a centuries-old tradition that brings the streets to life with music and light.

Esala Perahera

July/August. Ten days of grand processions with over 100 decorated elephants, dancers, and drummers.

Vesak Poya

May. Commemorating Buddha's birth, enlightenment, and death with elaborate lanterns and oil lamps.

Poson Poya

June. Marking the arrival of Buddhism in Sri Lanka, celebrated with spiritual devotion across the city.

Duruthu Perahera

January. A significant Buddhist pageant marking the first visit of the Buddha to Sri Lanka.

Visitor Essentials

Planning Your Visit

Temple Hours

Open daily from 5:30 AM to 8:00 PM. Puja ceremonies occur at 5:30 AM, 9:30 AM, and 6:30 PM.

Entry Fees

Foreign visitors pay an entrance fee (approx $15) which includes a guide booklet and footwear storage.

Getting to Kandy

The scenic "Udarata Menike" train from Colombo offers one of the world's most beautiful rail journeys.

Temple Etiquette

Remove shoes and hats. Shoulders and knees must be covered. Silence is required in the inner sanctum.

Best Time

The Esala Perahera in August is most vibrant; December to April offers the best weather for exploring.

Nearby Attractions

Peradeniya Royal Botanical Gardens, Bahirawakanda Buddha Statue, and the Udawatte Kele Sanctuary.
